5. Expected returns: what the portfolio should earn from here

A portfolio can be well-constructed around genuine return sources and still disappoint if those return sources are expensively priced at the point of entry. Knowing what a portfolio holds is not the same as knowing what it should earn. The two questions require different tools, and the second is where investment processes most often become imprecise — not because the math is difficult but because the assumptions embedded in a return estimate are rarely made explicit.

This section addresses that problem directly. It establishes the arithmetic structure of expected returns across asset classes, connects that arithmetic to macro regime dynamics, and develops the carry concept that links return expectations across equities, fixed income, and real assets in a multi-asset portfolio.

The arithmetic of expected returns

The most useful starting point for equity return estimation is a decomposition that forces the investor to state which element is actually being relied upon. Long-run equity return can be approximated as:

\[\text{Return} \approx \text{Starting yield} + \text{Real earnings growth} + \text{Inflation} + \text{Change in valuation multiple}\]

This is the Arnott and Bernstein decomposition (Arnott & Bernstein, 2002). Each component is observable or estimable independently. Starting yield is the current dividend yield — observable today. Real earnings growth per share is estimable from long-run productivity and demographic trends — typically 1 to 2 percent annually over long periods. Expected inflation is the market’s priced expectation of the price level — readable from inflation-linked bond breakevens. The multiple change is the residual: the repricing contribution that cannot be known in advance but can be bounded by asking what the starting valuation implies about its likely direction.

The value of this decomposition is not precision — it will not tell you what equity markets will return next year. Its value is that it makes hidden assumptions visible. The most common hidden assumption is that the valuation multiple will remain stable or continue expanding. If the starting CAPE is 30x, the current dividend yield is 1.5 percent, and long-run real earnings growth is 1.5 percent, the arithmetic from those two observable components is approximately 3 percent real before any multiple change. If the investor’s working assumption is 7 percent real, the remaining 4 percent must come from continued multiple expansion — an assumption that should be defended explicitly rather than imported from historical averages where conditions were different.

For high-quality fixed income the arithmetic is simpler. If a bond is held to maturity and does not default, the total nominal return equals the yield at purchase. At shorter horizons mark-to-market returns deviate as rates change, but the starting yield exerts a gravitational pull that strengthens as the holding period extends. An investor who allocated to ten-year Treasuries in mid-2020 at 0.5 percent yield, on the basis of the prior decade’s realized bond returns, was conflating one-time capital gains from a four-decade yield compression cycle with repeatable carry. When yields subsequently rose sharply in 2022, long-duration bonds fell 30–35 percent — equity-like losses from an instrument classified as low-risk. The yield was always telling the investor what to expect. The mistake was not reading it.

How macro regimes change the arithmetic

Starting conditions provide the raw material for a return estimate. The macro regime determines whether that estimate is realized. The same equity valuation multiple produces very different realized returns depending on whether it is entered in a disinflationary growth regime, an inflationary regime, or a credit-stress regime — because the discount rate applied to future cash flows changes materially across each.

Cochrane’s work establishes that most of the time-series variation in aggregate equity returns is explained by variation in discount rates rather than by variation in expected cash flow growth (Cochrane, 2011). A regime change — a sustained shift in inflation expectations, in central bank policy, or in the risk appetite of the marginal investor — changes the discount rate applied to every future cash flow in the portfolio simultaneously. This is why regime transitions tend to dominate medium-horizon realized returns in ways that starting valuation alone cannot predict.

Four regime conditions recur with sufficient regularity to frame any multi-asset return estimate.

Disinflationary growth — the dominant regime of 1985–2020 — is characterized by positive real GDP growth, falling or stable inflation, and declining or stable real interest rates. In this regime equities benefit from both earnings growth and multiple expansion, bonds benefit from capital appreciation as yields fall, and gold tends to underperform financial assets. This is the regime in which 60/40 portfolios performed best and in which most contemporary return estimates were calibrated.

Inflationary regime — present episodically through the 1970s and returning in 2021–2022 — is characterized by rising CPI, rising real interest rates, and compressed equity multiples as the discount rate applied to future earnings increases. Equities with high pricing power and real earnings growth outperform; long-duration bonds experience capital losses; gold and real assets tend to outperform financial assets. The 2022 episode showed clearly that a portfolio calibrated to the disinflationary regime’s return arithmetic will be wrong in a predictable direction when the regime shifts.

Equity bear and risk-off — episodically but repeatedly — is characterized by sharp equity declines, flight to high-quality sovereign bonds, and elevated volatility. In these periods equities lose capital, bonds may provide hedging value if the bear is deflationary, and gold tends to serve as a store of value when both equities and bonds are under pressure simultaneously. The duration of the defensive episode and the cause of it determine which assets provide genuine offset.

Neutral or ambiguous — which describes the majority of trading days, as the regime decomposition in section 7 shows (65% of the sample) — is the regime where none of the above conditions dominate and return is driven by the accumulated carry across the portfolio’s positions rather than by regime-level repricing. In this regime the starting yield and carry of each position is the primary driver of return, and the portfolio benefits from the breadth and independence of its return sources.

Carry as the cross-asset expected return signal

Carry is the return an investor earns from holding an asset if nothing changes — if prices remain flat, if yields stay where they are, if spot rates do not move. It is the compensation for time rather than for prediction, and it exists in different forms across every major asset class.

In equities, carry is the earnings yield — the inverse of the price-to-earnings ratio — adjusted for the expected payout and reinvestment rate. A market with a 5 percent earnings yield where earnings are paid out and reinvested at that yield offers 5 percent carry before any multiple change. In fixed income, carry is simply the yield minus the cost of financing, which for unlevered investors is the yield itself. In gold and silver, carry is negative in the traditional sense — there is no coupon — but positive in the sense that both assets provide optionality against tail scenarios in financial markets, an insurance premium whose value is context-dependent rather than continuous.

Asness, Moskowitz, and Pedersen show that carry — defined consistently across asset classes — is one of the most persistent return predictors available (Asness et al., 2013). Across eight asset classes and a sample spanning decades, high-carry assets have systematically outperformed low-carry assets. The mechanism is intuitive: carry is compensation for bearing the discomfort of holding what others are reluctant to own. When yield is high relative to its history, the asset is offering above-average compensation for its risk. When yield is compressed, the opposite holds.

The practical implication for a multi-asset portfolio is that the portfolio’s aggregate carry — the blended expected return from all positions assuming no price change — is the most honest single-number summary of what the portfolio should earn in neutral conditions. It is not the return that will be achieved, because regimes change and prices move. But it is the return the portfolio is being paid to wait for, and a portfolio whose aggregate carry is low or negative has a structural problem that higher expected alpha cannot reliably compensate for.

Working in ranges

Any honest return estimate must acknowledge substantial uncertainty. The practical response is not to produce a single number with apparent precision, but to construct a scenario range that identifies which assumption is doing most of the work — and what failure of that assumption would imply.

In equities the three drivers that most commonly separate the base case from the adverse case are the path of real earnings growth, the level at which the valuation multiple stabilizes, and the trajectory of real interest rates. In fixed income the equivalent is the path of inflation and the level at which policy rates settle. For each scenario the arithmetic identity provides the structure: return is yield plus growth plus multiple change, and stating the scenario means stating what values those components are assumed to take.

The most significant errors in expected return work rarely come from the arithmetic. They come from assumptions that are never stated — that the structural conditions underlying the forecast will remain roughly stable. Diversification relationships are assumed supportive because they recently were. A strategy with smooth realized history is assumed to continue smoothly because its tail risk has not yet been expressed. In all these cases the investor is importing a view about structural continuity that has not been examined as an assumption. The improvement that matters most is not more elaborate modeling — it is more honest accounting of which assumptions are carrying the forecast, and what their failure would imply for the stated return.
